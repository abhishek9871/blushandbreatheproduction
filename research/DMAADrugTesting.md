## DMAA Drug Testing: Detection Windows, Methods, and Procedures

### Detection Windows by Test Type

**Urine Testing**

DMAA detection in urine depends on the testing methodology and individual metabolism. Pharmacokinetic studies show that DMAA has a terminal half-life of approximately **8.4 hours** in plasma. Research using liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS-MS) has documented DMAA detection in urine for up to **105 hours (approximately 4-5 days)** after administration of a 40 mg dose. More conservative estimates suggest detection windows of **29-105 hours** depending on the dose, metabolism, individual physiology, and testing methodology employed.[1][2][3]

Peak DMAA concentration in the bloodstream typically occurs **3-5 hours after oral ingestion**, with mean plasma levels around 70 ng/mL following a 25 mg dose.[2][3]

**Blood Testing**

DMAA detection in blood is limited to a much shorter window. Based on pharmacokinetic analysis with an 8.4-hour half-life, DMAA in blood is typically detectable for approximately **24-48 hours** after ingestion at therapeutic or supplement doses. Specialized analysis using high-performance liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry (HPLC-MS) is required for blood testing, as standard immunoassays do not directly detect DMAA in plasma. At peak concentration (~70 ng/mL), samples would fall below detection thresholds within one to two full half-lives (roughly 17-25 hours).[3][2]

**Hair Testing**

Hair follicle tests can theoretically detect DMAA for up to **90 days** based on standard hair testing protocols, as drugs entering the bloodstream become incorporated into growing hair. However, there is a notable **5-7 day lag** after ingestion before DMAA metabolites appear in detectable quantities in hair. This delay occurs because drugs must enter the bloodstream and incorporate into the hair matrix as it grows. Hair testing protocols typically use a 1.5-inch hair sample from the scalp, which corresponds to approximately 90 days of hair growth at an average rate of 0.5 inches per month.[4][5]

### Immunoassay vs. GC-MS Confirmation Differences

**Immunoassay (Screening)**

Immunoassay testing, primarily using the **EMIT II Plus Amphetamines Assay**, is the initial screening method. The standard **cutoff level for SAMHSA-compliant testing is 500 ng/mL**, though immunoassays can be calibrated at 300, 500, or 1,000 ng/mL depending on the laboratory. Immunoassays use antibodies that bind to structurally similar compounds, making them **prone to cross-reactivity**.[6]

DMAA causes false positives on amphetamine immunoassays because its molecular structure is similar enough to amphetamine that the antibodies bind to it. Research shows that DMAA can trigger positive results on certain immunoassay kits at concentrations as low as **5 micrograms/mL on some assays**, though this varies by manufacturer and antibody specificity. The Syva EMIT II Plus assay, one of the most commonly used in military and employer testing, shows particular susceptibility to DMAA cross-reactivity.[7]

**Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS) Confirmation**

GC-MS is the gold standard for confirmation testing and provides definitive results. Unlike immunoassays, GC-MS **separates chemical compounds into unique molecular fragments** that are then matched to a database. Each drug has a distinctive fragmentation pattern, making GC-MS highly specific. Critically, GC-MS **will not produce a positive result for methamphetamine or amphetamine when only DMAA is present**. The confirmatory cutoff for amphetamine/methamphetamine by GC-MS is typically **100 ng/mL**, which is more sensitive than the immunoassay screening cutoff of 500 ng/mL.[8][9][10]

The two-step testing process—immunoassay screening followed by GC-MS confirmation for positives—is designed to eliminate false positives. If the immunoassay is positive but the GC-MS is negative, the test is reported as negative with no further action.[8]

### False Positive Rates and Documentation

**Overall False Positive Rate**

Research indicates that **false positives occur in approximately 5-10% of all drug tests**, with some studies reporting false negative rates of 10-15%. For amphetamine testing specifically, rates vary significantly by laboratory and testing methodology.[11]

**DMAA's Documented Impact on Military Testing**

The Department of Defense identified DMAA as a major cause of false-positive amphetamine results. A landmark DoD study analyzed 134 false-positive amphetamine samples from military drug testing—cases where immunoassay screening was positive but GC-MS confirmation was negative. LC-MS-MS analysis revealed DMAA in **92.3% of these false-positive samples at approximately 6.0 mg/L (6,000 ng/mL) concentration**. Another study documented that **124 out of 134 false-positive amphetamine samples (92.5%) in DoD testing contained DMAA**.[12][8]

This finding was so significant that the military, after conducting studies beginning in Spring 2010, formally identified DMAA supplement use as a source of false positives in the Army Drug Testing Program. The DoD subsequently prohibited DMAA for use by Service Members in April 2013, when the FDA declared DMAA illegal for dietary supplement use.[13][14]

**Laboratory-Specific False Positive Data**

In a comprehensive analysis of 3,571 urine specimens screened by the EMIT II immunoassay, results showed:
- 11.5% (412 samples) screened positive and confirmed positive by LC-MS-MS
- 88.5% (3,159 samples) screened negative
- **10.9% (389 samples) screened positive but failed confirmatory testing—false positives**[15]

A separate emergency department study of 10,011 urine drug screens revealed that among 362 samples (3.6%) positive for amphetamine by immunoassay, only 65% (234 samples) confirmed positive by GC-MS, meaning **35% were false positives**.[16]

### Military Drug Testing Specifics (DoD Protocols)

**Testing Procedure**

The Military Personnel Drug Abuse Testing Program (MPDATP), governed by DoD Instruction 1010.16, establishes standardized procedures across all military branches. The process includes:[17]

1. **Collection**: Urine samples are collected under observation, sealed with chain-of-custody documentation[18]
2. **Initial Screening**: Samples undergo immunoassay screening using approved reagents[17]
3. **Rescreening**: Any positive results are rescreened to confirm the positive[18]
4. **Confirmatory Testing**: Positive samples undergo gas chromatography/mass spectrometry (GC-MS) to identify specific substances[18]
5. **Cutoff Thresholds**: If GC-MS identifies a drug below the specified confirmation cutoff concentration, the test is reported as negative[18]

**DoD Prohibited Dietary Supplements**

DMAA is on the DoD Prohibited Dietary Supplement Ingredients list and is prohibited for use by all Service Members. This policy reflects DMAA's status as a readiness risk and its association with adverse events.[14]

**Disputing a Positive Result in the Military**

Service members facing a positive drug test result have several potential defenses:

- **False Positives Due to Supplements or Medications**: If a service member can document legitimate use of DMAA-containing supplements or other substances known to cause false positives, this constitutes valid grounds for challenging the result[19]
- **Chain of Custody Errors**: Procedural errors in collection, labeling, or transportation can compromise test integrity and invalidate results[19]
- **Laboratory Errors or Contamination**: Cross-contamination or equipment malfunction can lead to erroneous conclusions. Service members can request retesting of the original sample[19]
- **Procedural Violations**: Improper sample collection or testing procedures that violate DoD standards may invalidate results[19]

Service members with more than **6 years of active duty (enlisted) or 3 years after commissioning (officers)** can **demand a formal board hearing** to contest the results. Those with less time in service can provide a written rebuttal to the separation authority with exculpatory evidence such as alternative hair follicle testing or affidavits establishing their whereabouts and activities during the alleged use period.[20]

The command will routinely investigate claims of false positives, and service members should gather documentation including medical records, prescriptions, and evidence of procedural errors.[19]

### Employer Drug Testing Standards

**Testing Requirements and Procedures**

Employers using workplace drug testing programs follow SAMHSA (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration) guidelines and applicable state/federal law. The standard screening cutoff remains **500 ng/mL for amphetamines**, with confirmatory GC-MS testing mandatory for all positive results.[21][6]

Employers must:

1. **Use a Medical Review Officer (MRO)**: An MRO is a licensed physician certified to review all positive results and evaluate legitimate medical explanations[22][23]
2. **Provide Notification**: Employees must be informed of positive results and given the opportunity to explain the findings[24][25]
3. **Maintain Confidentiality**: Results are strictly confidential and separate from personnel records[21]
4. **Allow Confirmation Testing**: Employees can request confirmatory retesting of the original sample[25][11]

**MRO Review Process**

When a positive result is reported, the MRO contacts the employee to determine whether there is a valid prescription, medical reason, or legitimate explanation for the finding. If the MRO determines a legitimate medical explanation exists for the positive result—such as documented use of a DMAA-containing supplement—the MRO may **overturn the laboratory's initial finding and report the result as negative**. The MRO may also **order additional tests** if a potential legitimate cause is identified.[26][23]

If an employee provides evidence of using a DMAA-containing pre-workout supplement, weight loss product, or energy supplement and can demonstrate that use with receipts or testimonials, this constitutes a legitimate explanation for a positive amphetamine immunoassay.[21]

**False Positive Defenses in Employment**

Employees who believe they have tested positive due to a false positive should:[24]
- Document regular consumption of any potentially interfering substances before the test
- Provide prescriptions or purchase receipts for supplements
- Contact the MRO directly to discuss medications and supplements
- Request that a confirmatory GC-MS test be conducted if the immunoassay was positive

### What to Do If You Test Positive

**Immediate Steps**

1. **Remain Calm and Assess**: Don't panic. Understand that a positive immunoassay screening is not a final result. Approximately **10-35% of positive immunoassay results fail confirmatory testing**.[15][16]

2. **Request a Copy of Results**: Obtain written documentation of the test result and which testing method was used (immunoassay, GC-MS, or both).[11][24]

3. **Document Your Substance Use**: Compile a list of **all prescription medications, over-the-counter drugs, dietary supplements, and foods** consumed in the days before testing. Include dosages, dates, and brand names. Retain receipts and packaging.[27][24]

4. **Identify Potential Interfering Substances**: DMAA is notably documented to cause false positives on amphetamine tests. Other common culprits include:
   - Pseudoephedrine and phenylephrine (decongestants)
   - Bupropion (antidepressant)
   - Ephedrine and other stimulants[27]

5. **Contact the MRO**: If your test involved MRO review (common in employment and DOT testing), contact the MRO directly and provide a complete medication/supplement history. The MRO has authority to order additional testing or overturn positive results based on legitimate explanations.[23][24]

**Confirmatory Testing**

- **Request GC-MS Confirmation**: If only an immunoassay was performed, request that the split sample or original sample undergo GC-MS confirmation. This is your fundamental right in most testing programs[24][21]
- **Outcome**: If GC-MS is negative (does not detect amphetamine/methamphetamine), the positive immunoassay result should be reported as **negative** and dismissed[9][8]

**Formal Dispute Process**

- **Employment Setting**: Follow your employer's established procedure for contesting results. Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), employers must follow adverse action procedures if using results from a consumer reporting agency. You have the right to explain the result[24]
- **Military Setting**: If subject to military discipline, gather evidence of the false positive, document your use of potentially interfering substances, and file a formal rebuttal. Request a board hearing if eligible (6+ years enlisted service, 3+ years officer service)[20][19]
- **DOT Regulated Industries**: Request MRO reconsideration and provide medical documentation of supplement use. You may also seek legal counsel to challenge testing procedures[24]

**Legal and Support Resources**

- **Seek Legal Counsel**: If facing employment termination or military discipline, consult an employment lawyer or military law specialist[24][19]
- **Request Rehabilitation or EAP**: Some employers offer Employee Assistance Programs (EAP) or rehabilitation opportunities as alternatives to termination. Even in zero-tolerance environments, first-time positive results sometimes result in counseling and monitoring rather than immediate termination[28]
- **State Variations**: Your state may have specific legal protections. For example, Vermont prohibits termination for a first positive result if the employee agrees to seek treatment. Iowa requires employees to have the opportunity to explain results before action is taken[24]

**Documentation to Preserve**

- Purchase receipts for supplements containing DMAA
- Supplement packaging or labels showing ingredients
- Medical records if applicable
- Prescriptions for any medications taken
- Dates and times of consumption
- Witness statements of your activities and whereabouts
- Any prior correspondence with medical professionals about supplement use
- Chain of custody documentation from the testing laboratory
- Records of the specific immunoassay kits used
- Evidence of procedural errors in collection or handling

### Key Takeaways

DMAA cross-reactivity on amphetamine immunoassays is well-documented and scientifically established, with the military experiencing false-positive rates as high as 92% in samples later proven to contain only DMAA.
